# 13. How Engineers Manage Up

> "Managing up" means actively shaping your relationship with your manager.
> Not waiting to be evaluated, but designing the relationship.

> [🇰🇷 한국어](../foundations.ko/13_엔지니어의_매니저_관리.md)

---

## Why It Matters

Engineers often believe "if I do good work, recognition will follow."
In practice, **work that isn't visible doesn't exist.**
Your manager is juggling dozens of priorities, and what you don't surface, they don't know.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Understand Your Manager's World

Your manager also has their own boss, OKRs, and political pressure.
- Ask directly: "What's the most important thing for our team this quarter?"
- Adopt the perspective that your manager's success = your success.
- Know that there are things your manager can't say (→ `12_what_managers_cant_say.md`).

### 2. Make Your Work Visible

Good work that goes unannounced is as if it didn't happen.
- In 1:1s, don't just report status — mention meaningful progress.
- "This week I resolved [X], and I think it'll have an impact on [Y]."
- Talk about impact, not just completion.

### 3. Bring Problems With Solutions

Someone who only brings problems looks different from someone who brings options.
- Not "here's a problem" but "here's a problem, and I've thought through A and B. What do you think?"
- Let your manager make the call, but do the thinking first.

### 4. Use 1:1s Strategically

1:1s are **your time**, not your manager's.
- Don't fill them with status reports — that belongs in Slack or a doc.
- Bring questions, challenges, and career conversations.
- "What should I focus on most for my growth this quarter?"

### 5. Give Feedback Through the Right Channel at the Right Time

Feedback through formal channels (performance review systems) becomes a record — it can be read differently than you intended.
- Give feedback to your manager **in a 1:1 first**.
- Choose a calm moment. Avoid right after emotions have spiked.
- Use the language of "it would help me if..." (request, not criticism).

### 6. State Your Career Goals Clearly

Your manager is not a mind reader. If you don't say it, they don't know.
- "I want to reach senior by end of year. What's the biggest gap I need to close?"
- When you name your goal, your manager looks for opportunities to connect you to it.
- When you don't, they assume you're satisfied with where you are.

### 7. Trust Comes From Reliability

**Predictability** builds trust more than capability.
- Keep your commitments. If you can't, say so in advance.
- "Surprise issues" erode trust — share risks early.
- Keeping small promises is the foundation of large trust.

### 8. Escalate Carefully

Going to a skip-level or HR is a nuclear option.
- Try to resolve it directly with your immediate manager first.
- If you want a skip-level meeting, tell your manager first.
- Understand that relationships after escalation are hard to repair.

## When You Have a Difficult Manager

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| No feedback | Ask directly: "What am I doing well, and where should I develop?" |
| Over-controlling | "If I understand what outcome you're looking for, could I decide how to get there?" |
| Feels unfair | Bring specific examples to a 1:1. Speak about behavior, not emotion. |
| Doesn't communicate | Your manager may have constraints. See `12_what_managers_cant_say.md`. |
| You received a PIP | Understand the content clearly and get the success criteria in writing. Don't react emotionally. |
